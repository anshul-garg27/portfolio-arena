Anshull Garg is an Indian software developer currently employed at Walmart Global Tech, where he works on large-scale distributed systems serving millions of users. He is known in professional circles for building scalable backend architectures and for his persistent belief that "one more refactor" will finally make the codebase perfect.[1]

Garg has been described as a "full-stack engineer with backend tendencies"[citation needed] and has contributed to systems processing significant transaction volumes at Walmart. His work primarily involves designing and implementing services that operate at the intersection of high availability, low latency, and occasionally unreasonable deadlines.[2]

Early life and education edit

Anshull Garg was born in India, where he demonstrated an early aptitude for taking things apart and not always putting them back together[citation needed]. Sources close to the subject report that his first interaction with a computer was "formative, if not slightly obsessive."[3]

He pursued formal education in computer science, during which he reportedly discovered that programming could be both a vocation and a reliable source of existential frustration. Peers from this period describe him as "the person you asked for help debugging at 2 AM"[citation needed] -- a role he apparently accepted with minimal complaint and maximal caffeine consumption.

During his academic career, Garg participated in competitive programming and hackathons, an experience that scholars have noted instills either a deep love of algorithms or a permanent aversion to sleep deprivation, and in many cases both simultaneously.[4]

Career edit

Early career edit

Garg began his professional career with a focus on backend development and systems design, quickly establishing a pattern that would define his trajectory: identifying bottlenecks, proposing solutions, and then building those solutions before anyone had time to form a committee about them.[5]

In his early roles, he gained experience with microservices architecture, RESTful API design, and the peculiar art of explaining to product managers why "just adding one more feature" is never as simple as it sounds. His contributions during this period included performance optimizations, database schema redesigns, and what colleagues diplomatically referred to as "aggressive code reviews."[citation needed]

Walmart Global Tech edit

This section describes the subject's current employment. Information may be subject to corporate NDAs and strategic omission.

Garg joined Walmart Global Tech as a Software Developer, where he works on e-commerce platform engineering and large-scale distributed systems. Walmart's technology division employs thousands of engineers globally and serves over 240 million customers weekly,[6] a scale that Garg has described as "humbling, and occasionally terrifying during Black Friday."[citation needed]

At Walmart, his responsibilities include the design and implementation of backend services, performance optimization of high-traffic systems, and contributing to the platform architecture that supports one of the world's largest retailers. He has worked across multiple domains including inventory management, order processing, and internal tooling -- systems that, by their nature, must fail gracefully or not at all.[7]

Colleagues at Walmart have noted Garg's ability to navigate complex legacy codebases, a skill that the academic literature on software maintenance describes as "part archaeology, part diplomacy."[8] His contributions to the organization have reportedly improved system reliability and developer productivity, though the exact metrics remain proprietary and, frankly, the subject of some internal debate.

Notable works edit

Main articles: Distributed Systems at Walmart, E-commerce Platform Engineering

Garg's portfolio of work spans several categories of software engineering, each of which would arguably merit its own Wikipedia article if the notability threshold were somewhat more flexible.

Scalable Backend Services -- Garg has designed and built backend services handling high-throughput workloads. These systems, described in internal documentation as "the pipes that keep the water running," employ event-driven architectures, distributed caching, and asynchronous processing patterns.[9] The services reportedly maintain sub-100ms response times under peak load, a claim that is either impressive or a rounding error depending on one's perspective.[citation needed]

Platform Infrastructure & Tooling -- A recurring theme in Garg's work is the development of internal tools that improve developer velocity. These include CI/CD pipeline optimizations, automated testing frameworks, and monitoring dashboards. His philosophy on tooling has been summarized as: "If you have to do it more than twice, automate it. If you have to do it once, consider automating it anyway."[10]

API Design & Integration -- Garg has contributed to the design of APIs connecting multiple internal and external systems. His approach to API design is reportedly influenced by the RESTful principles outlined by Roy Fielding,[11] combined with a pragmatic willingness to use GraphQL when the situation warrants it, which he has described as "not a betrayal, but an evolution."

Philosophy and approach edit

Garg's engineering philosophy, as synthesized from his public writings, code reviews, and reportedly passionate Slack messages, can be organized into several core tenets:

On simplicity: Garg has consistently advocated for simplicity in system design, a position that has placed him in occasional tension with proponents of more elaborate architectures. He has been quoted as saying, "The best code is the code you don't have to write,"[13] a sentiment attributed to various luminaries but which Garg applies with what observers describe as "unusual sincerity."

On reliability: Drawing from the Site Reliability Engineering tradition popularized by Google,[14] Garg emphasizes observability, graceful degradation, and comprehensive error handling. He reportedly considers unhandled exceptions to be a form of "technical debt with interest."

On collaboration: Garg views software development as an inherently collaborative discipline and has been described as an active participant in code reviews, architecture discussions, and the occasional heated debate about tabs versus spaces (he reportedly has strong opinions on this matter, the details of which are omitted here to preserve editorial neutrality).

"The measure of a good engineer is not the code they write, but the code they prevent from being written." -- Attributed to Anshull Garg, though the provenance of this quote is disputed.[15]

On continuous learning: Garg maintains a practice of exploring emerging technologies and design patterns, a habit that his peers describe as "commendable and occasionally disruptive" when it results in proposals to rewrite existing systems in newer frameworks.[citation needed]

Critical reception of Garg's work has been broadly positive, though as with all biographical articles, the following assessments should be read with an awareness of selection bias.

-- Engineering colleague, Walmart Global Tech[16]
"One of the most thorough code reviewers I've worked with. Sometimes too thorough, if I'm being honest."
-- Anonymous peer (identity withheld at their request)[17]
"Anshull doesn't just solve the problem in front of him -- he solves the three problems that would have come next. It's annoying how often he's right about that."
-- Former team lead[18]

A quantitative analysis of his professional trajectory is complicated by the proprietary nature of his primary employer's metrics. However, proxies such as peer recognition, scope of responsibilities, and the number of Slack channels in which he is a "required member" suggest a pattern of expanding influence.[citation needed]

His work has been described as "widely regarded as exceptional"[citation needed] by individuals whose objectivity may reasonably be questioned, as they include close colleagues, former mentors, and in at least one instance, the subject himself during a moment of uncharacteristic self-promotion.

Personal life edit

Garg maintains a private personal life, consistent with the general expectations of the Wikipedia Biographies of Living Persons policy. He is known to reside in India and to have interests outside of software engineering, including but not limited to activities that do not involve staring at a screen.[citation needed]

Colleagues report that Garg is an avid learner who spends a nontrivial portion of his free time reading technical literature, contributing to open-source projects, and occasionally attempting to explain distributed consensus algorithms to people who did not ask.[19]
